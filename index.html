Why Founders and Coders

I'm trying to think of the first time I encountered coding, and it must go all the way to high school, when we were taught basics of Logo. At the time I found it almost too easy and too... simple - drawing circles and squares has a limited scope, after all. This was well before the time of Scratch; the impression I got back then was that coding was the domain of skilled mathematicians and definitely not something anybody off the street can do.

I used to work for a charity called SCA. As a charity, it mainly relies on grants to fund its projects - it also has a supplementary income which is DBS (criminal record check) processing. The only way of seeing if a certificate was issued, was to go to a particular website, input the form code and some other information. My lovely boss was doing it manually without complaining. She was away one week - a well deserved holiday - and I decided to help out and do some of the tracking in her absence. Suffice to say that within 10 minutes I started to think how to automate the process. With the help from a friend, I built a little script which cut down human involvement of the process from hours to minutes. When I demonstrated the script in action, my boss thought it was magic - she wondered how I knew this could be done. I think at the time my logic was limited to: it's repetitive and involves computers; thus it should be possible to automate it!

I spoke with SCA's boss recently, and she disclosed that the charity has been able to continue taking on new clients for this service, and that this would not have been possible without the little script and the difference it makes in human time needed for the process. I am hoping to expand the functionality of the script to deal with potential errors, update it so it deals with more modern MS Office files, and add a GUI.

At SCA I also acted as the IT Officer and the webmaster; this, along with the little programming I did, roused my interest in pursuing career - in one or the other aspect of IT. My experience at SCA allowed me to transition to a job which I hoped would allow me to expand on my IT Support knowledge. As it became clear that the support would be generally limited to turn-it-off-and-on, I decided to explore ways of expanding my knowledge to help with my next career step.

I signed up for and am currently doing a part-time, distance learning, Computer Sciences MSc. However, I found, it is severely lacking not only in modern practices in curriculum, but also practical projects.

Earlier this year I took the opportunity to join a small start-up for a short training contract as a trainee developer. I learned how to create a basic to-do list website using Django framework, to create website to a design using CSS, jquery, and bootstrap. This experience reassured me that I enjoy programming and that it is something I want to do career wise.

Having since done some of the FreeCodeCamp exercises, I realised I learn best by doing, and I hope joining Founders & Coders will be a perfect opportunity to gain practical skills and build up a portfolio of projects. As I still consider myself to be a beginner when it come to programming (possibly due to the fact that a significant proportion of my friends do coding for living), I know that I need a learning opportunity which is structured, which encourages questions, and one that has well defined aims and objectives for each step of progression - all of which Founders and Coders provides.

Long term objectives

  • make a difference
  • streamline and optimise processes
  • help small businesses and small charities thrive by bringing them into the 21st century
